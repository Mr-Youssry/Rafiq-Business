# Legal & Regulatory Environment - USA

## Overview
The United States has a robust but complex regulatory framework for education technology, with both federal laws and state-specific regulations that EdTech companies must navigate. For foreign founders, business formation is straightforward, but compliance requirements are extensive.

## Federal Privacy Laws

### FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)
**Status:** Enacted 1974, actively enforced

**Key Provisions:**
- Protects privacy of students' education records
- Bars disclosure of personally identifiable information (PII) in student records to third parties without parental consent
- Applies to all schools and districts receiving federal funding (virtually all public K-12 schools)
- Parents and students 18+ can access records, request changes, and control disclosure

**Implications for Rafiq AI:**
- Must obtain proper consent before accessing student data
- Cannot use student data for commercial purposes (marketing, sales)
- Must have strict data handling protocols
- Schools will require detailed data privacy agreements

**Enforcement:**
- Violations can result in: significant fines, loss of federal funding, disciplinary action from US Department of Education

### COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act)
**Status:** Enacted 1998, amended 2025

**Key Provisions:**
- Protects privacy of children under 13
- Managed by Federal Trade Commission (FTC)
- Requires parental consent for collection/use of children's personal information
- Applies to websites, online services, apps directed to children

**2025 Updates (Effective April 22, 2025):**
- Expanded definitions of "personal information" and "online contact information"
- New standards for "mixed audience" services
- Enhanced parental notice and consent requirements
- Stricter data retention and security obligations
- Greater safe harbor program transparency

**School Authorization Exception:**
- FTC did not codify guidance permitting schools to authorize collection on behalf of parents for EdTech services
- Will continue to enforce based on existing guidance
- Avoided changes that could conflict with upcoming FERPA regulation changes

**Implications for Rafiq AI:**
- If platform records student voices/videos (instructional coaching context), need to ensure COPPA compliance
- Schools may authorize on behalf of parents, but FTC guidance is under review
- Must implement enhanced data security measures
- Cannot collect more data than necessary
- Must delete data when no longer needed

**Recent Enforcement Example:**
- January 2025: Cognosphere (Genshin Impact) fined $20M for COPPA violations
- Required to block children under 16 from in-game purchases without consent
- Demonstrates active FTC enforcement

### PPRA (Protection of Pupil Rights Amendment)
**Key Provisions:**
- Governs surveys, analysis, evaluations funded by Department of Education
- Requires parental approval for many assessment tools
- Districts must have policies for data use

**Implications for Rafiq AI:**
- If using assessment/feedback surveys, ensure compliance with PPRA
- Work within existing district policies

## State-Level Privacy Laws

### Comprehensive State Privacy Landscape (2025)
- **19 states** now have comprehensive data privacy laws
- **8 new states** enacted laws in 2025: Delaware, Iowa, Maryland, Minnesota, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Tennessee
- **16 additional states** currently deliberating privacy bills (including Massachusetts, New York)

### Key State-Specific Regulations

#### California
**Education-Specific:**
- AB-1584 (2014) / CA Education Code Section 49073.1: Pupil records privacy for third-party contracts with digital storage/educational software
- AB-2799 (2016): Preschool/prekindergarten personal information privacy

**General Privacy (2025 Updates):**
- California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) proposed regulations for:
  - Cybersecurity audits
  - Risk assessments
  - Automated decision-making technology (ADMT)
- ADMT regulations give consumers right to know when automated decisions were made using their data and to opt-out
- **Relevant for AI:** Applies to education contexts
- Delete Act (SB 362): Takes effect August 2026, allows Californians to remove information from registered data brokers

**Implications:**
- California is largest K-12 market (most teachers, highest salaries)
- Strictest privacy requirements in US
- Must register as data broker if applicable
- Must provide opt-out for AI-driven decisions
- High compliance costs but critical market

#### New York
**Education-Specific:**
- Education Law SB 6356 (2014) with two parts:

  - ยง 2-d: Unauthorized release of PII
- Requirements:
  - High levels of encryption

  - Prohibits use for commercial purposes (marketing/sales)
  - Chief Privacy Officer position required
  - Parents' bill of rights for data privacy

**Part 121 Regulations:**
- Adoption of data security and privacy standard
- Creation of data security plans
- Designation of data protection officers for every educational agency

**Child-Specific (2024-2025):**
- New York Child Data Protection Act (NYCDPA) - Effective June 20, 2025
- Protects users under 18
- Cannot process personal data without consent (except "strictly necessary" purposes)

- Second largest market (NYC metro area)
- Very strict data requirements
- Must work with designated Chief Privacy Officers

#### Texas
**2025 Updates:**
- App Store Accountability Act: Requires age information collection, age bracket signals to developers
- Amended data broker law twice in 2025
- Must respond to universal opt-out mechanisms (Global Privacy Control) as of January 2025
- SCOPE Act (Securing Children Online through Parental Empowerment): Active enforcement

**Recent Enforcement:**
- December 2025: AG Ken Paxton probing 10+ tech companies (Discord, Reddit) for children's privacy violations

**Implications:**
- Large market with active enforcement
- Must implement opt-out mechanisms
- Strong focus on children's privacy

### State-by-State Variation Challenges
- Each state has different requirements
- No federal comprehensive privacy law (unlike GDPR)
- Must maintain compliance across multiple jurisdictions
- Recommended: Build to strictest standard (California) for scalability

## AI Regulations in Education

### Federal AI Policy (2025)

**Executive Order: "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth" (April 2025)**
- Goal: Enable youth to gain AI expertise from early age
- Established White House Task Force on Artificial Intelligence Education
- Director of Office of Science and Technology Policy serves as Chair

**US Department of Education Guidance:**
- Dear Colleague Letter on leveraging federal grant funds for AI
- Outlines how AI may be used across educational functions
- Articulates principles for responsible AI use
- Confirms AI uses allowable under existing federal education programs

**Recent Development (December 2025):**
- Executive Order aimed at blocking states from crafting own AI regulations
- Seeks to prevent "onerous" state regulations
- Directs federal agencies to pressure states via withholding funding or court challenges
- Goal: Create lighter-touch federal framework to override state laws
- **Impact:** Creates uncertainty but generally favorable for AI EdTech innovation

### State-Level AI Activity

**Requirements:**
- **Ohio and Tennessee:** Require districts to have comprehensive AI policy (only two states with mandates)

**State Guidance Released (2025):**
- Maine, Missouri, Nevada, New Mexico released AI guidelines for schools
- New Mexico AI Guidance for K-12 Education 1.0 (May 2025): Comprehensive guidance for responsible AI integration

**Legislation Introduced (2025):**
- At least 20 states introduced AI-related education bills
- Mississippi SB 2426 (enacted): Creates state AI task force
- California AB 1064, Connecticut SB 2, Texas HB 1709: Create oversight boards and "regulatory sandboxes" for AI testing

**Focus Areas (from state task forces):**
- Creating curricular frameworks for AI literacy
- Investing in educator professional development
- Ensuring equitable access
- Protecting student data
- Supporting districts with implementation

**Implications for Rafiq AI:**
- Federal push toward permissive AI regulation (favorable)
- State-level patchwork of requirements (challenging)
- Growing acceptance of AI in instructional contexts
- Strong focus on responsible use and data protection
- Opportunity: Position as "responsible AI" that helps teachers (not replaces them)

## Business Formation for Foreign Founders

### Delaware LLC Formation

**Why Delaware:**
- Over 2/3 of Fortune 500 companies registered in Delaware
- Only state with separate business law court (Court of Chancery)
- Over 100 years of legal precedent providing clear business roadmap
- Preferred by VCs and investors (critical for fundraising)

**Eligibility:**
- Anyone can establish Delaware LLC (except residents of Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia)
- No requirement to be US citizen or resident
- Can run company entirely from abroad
- Don't need to visit Delaware

**Privacy Benefits:**
- Delaware doesn't require LLCs to disclose member/manager information on public documents
- Offers anonymity and protection (unless required by law enforcement)
- Information about ownership not publicly available

**Key Requirements:**

1. **Registered Agent:**
   - Must have Registered Agent in Delaware
   - Can be individual resident or business entity authorized in Delaware
   - Must have physical street address in Delaware
   - Cost: Various service providers ($50-300/year)

2. **EIN (Employer Identification Number):**
   - Mandatory from IRS for business activities, bank account, hiring
   - Required for filing and paying US taxes
   - Foreign founders can obtain online

3. **Annual Tax:**
   - LLCs pay $300 annual tax (no annual report required)
   - Due March 1st each year

4. **BOI Report (Beneficial Ownership Information):**
   - Required as of March 26, 2025 for most non-US LLCs/corporations
   - File with Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)
   - Existing companies deadline: April 25, 2025
   - New businesses: 30 days from formation

5. **FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Report):**
   - Required if non-US person's business ever has $10,000+ connected to company
   - File with US Treasury

**Formation Timeline:**
- Standard: 2-5 business days
- Expedited options: Same-day or 24-hour (additional cost)

**Costs:**
- State filing fees: $110 (LLC) or $109 (Corporation)
- Registered agent: $50-300/year
- Total first year: ~$500-800

**LLC vs. Corporation:**
- **LLC:** Flexible, simpler for small businesses, pass-through taxation
- **Corporation (C-Corp):** Preferred for raising venture capital, issuing shares
- **Recommendation for Rafiq AI:** Start as Delaware C-Corp if planning to raise VC funding

## School District Procurement Requirements

### Procurement Process Characteristics

**Timeline:**
- Sales cycles: 6-18 months typical
- 59% of districts take 6+ months to make purchasing decision
- Nearly 1 in 5 take 18+ months
- Decisions align with academic calendars and budget years

**Stakeholders:**
- IT directors
- Curriculum specialists
- Administrators
- School boards
- Data privacy officers (in many states)

**Current Challenges (2025):**
- Sunsetting ESSER federal stimulus funds
- Shortage of time and capacity
- Rapidly evolving AI marketplace
- Budget constraints

### Compliance Requirements

**Data Privacy:**
- Must comply with FERPA, COPPA (federal)
- Must comply with state-specific laws
- Many districts require Standard Student Data Privacy Agreement (NDPA)
- Some states (California) use Ed Tech JPA requiring all vendors to adopt NDPA

**Procurement Standards:**
- Transparency and fairness requirements
- Technology purchases require additional scrutiny
- Alignment with curriculum goals
- Data security standards
- Budgetary constraints
- Often require pilot testing before full adoption

**Vendor Requirements:**
- Getting on approved vendor list
- Detailed product assessment rubrics
- Evidence-based product certifications aligned with federal standards
- Proof of effectiveness (student outcomes data)

### Best Practices for EdTech Vendors

1. **Standardized Process:** Develop materials that meet common district requirements
2. **Early Engagement:** Start 6-12 months before budget deadlines
3. **Budget Alignment:** Target spring/summer (K-12 budget planning periods)
4. **Cooperative Purchasing:** Leverage programs like Sourcewell, Ed Tech JPA
5. **Data Privacy First:** Lead with robust data privacy commitments
6. **Evidence-Based:** Provide research showing effectiveness

### 2025-26 Priority Areas
- Cybersecurity
- Data privacy and security
- Generative AI (major focus)

## Regulatory Risk Assessment

### High-Risk Areas
1. **Student Data Privacy:** Extremely strict enforcement, reputational risk

### Medium-Risk Areas
1. **Procurement Complexity:** Long sales cycles, multiple stakeholders
2. **School Authorization for COPPA:** FTC guidance under review
3. **Accessibility Requirements:** ADA compliance for EdTech

### Low-Risk Areas
1. **Business Formation:** Straightforward Delaware LLC/C-Corp process
2. **Federal AI Encouragement:** Current federal stance is permissive

## Overall Legal & Regulatory Score: 3/5

### Strengths
- Clear regulatory framework (well-established laws)
- Easy business formation for foreign founders
- Federal government encouraging AI in education
- Large, sophisticated legal/compliance service provider ecosystem

### Challenges
- Complex state-by-state variation (50 different requirements)
- Strict student data privacy enforcement (high stakes)
- Expensive compliance infrastructure required
- Uncertain AI regulatory landscape (federal vs. state tension)
- Long procurement cycles with heavy documentation requirements

### Verdict
**CAUTION:** Regulatory environment is manageable but requires significant upfront investment in compliance infrastructure and ongoing legal costs. Not a barrier to entry for well-capitalized startups, but challenging for bootstrapped operations. The clear benefit is that once compliance is achieved for the US market, it becomes a competitive moat against less compliant competitors.
